Pollution

The web content from Wikipedia provides a comprehensive overview of pollution, defined as the introduction of contaminants into the natural environment that cause harm. It details various types of pollution, their natural and human-generated sources, their effects on human health and the environment, and various methods for regulation, monitoring, and control.

Types of Pollution

Air Pollution

The release of chemicals and particulates into the atmosphere.

Contaminants:

  • Carbon monoxide (CO)
  • Sulfur dioxide (SO2)
  • Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)
  • Nitrogen oxides (NOx)
  • Photochemical ozone
  • Smog
  • Particulate matter (PM10 to PM2.5)

Water Pollution

Caused by discharge of wastewater, sewage, and contaminants into surface waters.

Contaminants:

  • Industrial wastewater
  • Untreated sewage
  • Chlorine
  • Chemical fertilizers
  • Pesticides
  • Human feces
  • Oil spills

Soil Contamination

Occurs when chemicals are released by spill or underground leakage.

Contaminants:

  • Hydrocarbons
  • Heavy metals (chromium, cadmium, lead)
  • MTBE
  • Herbicides
  • Pesticides
  • Chlorinated hydrocarbons
  • Dioxins (TCDD)

Radioactive Contamination

Resulting from activities in atomic physics like nuclear power generation and weapons.

Contaminants:

  • Alpha emitters
  • Actinides in the environment

Other Types of Pollution

  • Noise Pollution: Encompasses roadway noise, aircraft noise, industrial noise, and high-intensity sonar
  • Plastic Pollution: Involves the accumulation of plastic products and microplastics in the environment
  • Thermal Pollution: A temperature change in natural water bodies caused by human influence, such as using water as a coolant in power plants
  • Light Pollution: Includes light trespass, over-illumination, and astronomical interference
  • Visual Pollution: Presence of overhead power lines, motorway billboards, scarred landforms, open storage of trash, and space debris
  • Chemical Pollution: The introduction of novel entities (NEs) like per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) into the environment

Sources and Causes

Natural Sources

Volcanoes

Emissions: Carbon dioxide, hydrogen halides (causing acid rain), sulfur dioxide, hydrogen sulfide, toxic fine particles (arsenic, lead, mercury).

Wildfires

Emissions: Carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, fine particulates.

Human Sources

Point Source

Factories, mines, construction sites, oil refineries, chemical plants.

Nonpoint Source

Microplastics, agricultural runoff.

General Activities

  • Manufacturing
  • Extractive industries
  • Poor waste management
  • Transportation
  • Agriculture (clear felling, burning, pesticide/herbicide spraying)

Specific Sources

  • Motor vehicle emissions
  • Coal-fired power plants
  • Incinerators
  • Large livestock farms
  • Municipal landfills

Effects of Pollution

Human Health Effects

  • Ozone pollution: Respiratory disease, cardiovascular disease, throat inflammation, chest pain
  • Water pollution: Waterborne illnesses (e.g., diarrhoea)
  • Noise pollution: Hearing loss, high blood pressure, stress, sleep disturbance
  • Mercury poisoning: Developmental deficits in children, neurologic symptoms
  • Lead and heavy metals: Neurological problems, intellectual disabilities, behavioural problems
  • Chemical and radioactive substances: Cancer and birth defects

Environmental and Ecological Impacts

  • Biomagnification: Concentration of toxins like heavy metals in higher trophic levels
  • Ocean Acidification: Decrease in ocean pH due to CO2 dissolution
  • Global Warming: Greenhouse gas emissions affecting ecosystems
  • Ecosystem Alteration: Nitrogen oxides from acid rain fertilize land, changing species composition
  • Reduced Photosynthesis: Smog and haze block sunlight; tropospheric ozone damages plants
  • Soil Infertility: Contaminated soil becomes unsuitable for plants, disrupting the food web
  • Acid Rain: Caused by sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides, lowering soil pH
  • Water Deoxygenation (Hypoxia): Organic pollution depletes oxygen, reducing aquatic species diversity
  • Plastic Accumulation: Forms garbage patches (e.g., Great Pacific Garbage Patch), entangling and being ingested by wildlife

Control Measures

Practices

  • Recycling
  • Reusing
  • Waste minimisation
  • Mitigating
  • Pollution prevention
  • Compost

Technologies

Air Pollution Control Technologies

  • Green wall
  • Smog Tower
  • Thermal oxidizer
  • Dust collection systems (Baghouses, Cyclones, Electrostatic precipitators)
  • Scrubbers (Baffle spray, Cyclonic spray, Ejector venturi, etc.)

Water/Wastewater Treatment Technologies

  • Sedimentation
  • Activated sludge biotreaters
  • Aerated lagoons
  • Constructed wetlands
  • API oil-water separators
  • Biofilters
  • Dissolved air flotation (DAF)
  • Ultrafiltration

Other Technologies

  • Bioremediation
  • Vapor recovery systems
  • Phytoremediation

Statistics

Global Deaths

Pollution killed approximately nine million people worldwide in 2019 (one in six deaths).

Air Pollution Deaths

Air pollution caused about three-quarters of the nine million pollution-related deaths in 2019.

Water Pollution Deaths

Water pollution caused 1.4 million premature deaths in 2019.

Regional Statistics

  • China (2010): Estimated 1.2 million premature deaths
  • India (2019): 1.67 million deaths (17.8% of total deaths)
  • United States: Estimated over 50,000 annually

Waste Generation

About 400 million metric tons of hazardous wastes are generated each year. The U.S. produces about 250 million metric tons.

US Environmental Impact

Americans constitute less than 5% of the world's population but produce roughly 25% of the world's CO2 and generate approximately 30% of the world's waste.

Planetary Boundary

A 2022 study found that levels of anthropogenic chemical pollution have exceeded planetary boundaries and threaten entire ecosystems.

Historical Timeline

Pre-19th Century

Pollution from fires since prehistoric times; increased pollution from Greek, Roman, and Chinese metal production. King Edward I of England banned burning mineral coal in London in 1306.

19th Century

The Industrial Revolution gave birth to modern pollution. The 'Great Stink' on the Thames in 1858 led to the London sewerage system. Chicago and Cincinnati enacted clean air laws in 1881.

20th and 21st Century

  • 1940s: Automobile-caused smog became a significant issue in Los Angeles. Donora, Pennsylvania smog event.
  • 1952: Great Smog of London killed at least 4000 people, leading to the Clean Air Act of 1956.
  • 1950s-1970s: Major US environmental legislation passed: Noise Control Act, Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, National Environmental Policy Act.
  • 1974: EPA banned consumption of fish from Hudson River due to PCB dumping.
  • 1980: Superfund legislation passed in response to Love Canal and Valley of the Drums contamination.
  • 1984: Bhopal disaster.
  • 1987: Montreal Protocol signed to ban ozone-depleting chemicals.
  • 1997: Kyoto Protocol initiated collaboration on greenhouse gases.
  • 2001: Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants created.
  • 2007: China overtook the US as the world's biggest producer of CO2.
  • 2023: A Global Framework on Chemicals was agreed upon in Bonn, Germany.

Geographical Data

Top Air Polluters

China, United States, Russia, India, Mexico, and Japan are world leaders in air pollution emissions.

Most Polluted Places

  • Lake Karachay (Russia) named 'most polluted spot' by Worldwatch Institute
  • Chelyabinsk, Russia considered 'Most polluted place on the planet'

Access Statistics

  • Sanitation Access: Over 732 million Indians (56%) and over 92 million Ethiopians (92.9%) lack basic sanitation
  • Drinking Water Access: As of 2007, nearly 500 million Chinese lacked access to safe drinking water
