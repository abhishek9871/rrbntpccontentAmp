Environmental Science

Environmental science is an interdisciplinary academic field that integrates physics, biology, chemistry, and geography to study the environment and solve environmental problems. It emerged from natural history and medicine during the Enlightenment and became a substantive field in the 1960s and 1970s.

Definition

An interdisciplinary academic field that integrates physics, biology, meteorology, mathematics, and geography (including ecology, chemistry, plant science, zoology, mineralogy, oceanography, limnology, soil science, geology, physical geography, and atmospheric science) to the study of the environment and the solution of environmental problems.

Approach

Provides an integrated, quantitative, and interdisciplinary approach to the study of environmental systems.

Terminology Distinctions

Ecology

Technically refers only to the study of organisms and their interactions with each other and their environment. It can be considered a subset of environmental science.

Environmental Studies

Incorporates more of the social sciences to understand human relationships, perceptions, and policies towards the environment.

Environmental Engineering

Focuses on design and technology for improving environmental quality.

Key Components of Environmental Science

Atmospheric Sciences

Focuses on the Earth's atmosphere and its interrelation with other systems. Includes studies of meteorology, greenhouse gas phenomena, atmospheric dispersion modeling of airborne contaminants, sound propagation related to noise pollution, and light pollution. It involves physicists creating computer models, chemists examining atmospheric chemicals, and biologists analyzing carbon dioxide fluxes.

Ecology

The study of the relationships between living organisms, including humans, and their physical environment. It seeks to understand the vital connections between plants and animals and the world around them. Ecologists may investigate relationships between a population and a physical characteristic of their environment or interactions between different populations.

Environmental Chemistry

The study of chemical alterations in the environment. Principal areas of study include soil contamination and water pollution. Topics include chemical degradation, multi-phase transport of chemicals, and chemical effects on biota.

Geosciences

Includes environmental geology, environmental soil science, volcanic phenomena, and the evolution of the Earth's crust. In some systems, this can also include hydrology and oceanography. Studies can involve calculating soil erosion and sediment transport.

Historical Development

Ancient Civilizations

Concerns included deforestation (Mesopotamia, 2700 BCE), sanitation improvements by observing river systems (Indus Valley, 2500 BCE), and soil erosion from agriculture (Central America, 1500 BCE).

18th Century

In 1735, Carolus Linnaeus introduced binomial nomenclature to classify living organisms.

19th Century

Scientists studied atmospheric gases and their interaction with heat, leading to the discovery of the greenhouse effect and evidence of a past Ice Age.

20th Century

The discipline took its modern shape. Key events include the publication of Rachel Carson's 'Silent Spring' (1962), the 1969 Santa Barbara oil spill, the Cuyahoga River fire (1969), the first Earth Day (1970), and the formation of the US EPA (1970) and UN Environment Programme (1972). Other major events were the Love Canal disaster (1978), Three Mile Island accident (1979), Bhopal disaster (1984), discovery of the ozone hole (1985), and the Chernobyl disaster (1986).

21st Century

Focus on modeling climate change effects and promoting global cooperation. The Kyoto Protocol became the Paris Agreement in 2016. Marked by significant technological advancements including GIS, remote sensing, bioremediation, blockchain for fishery monitoring, thermal imaging for wildlife management, and AI for predicting animal population movements.

Important Milestones and Publications

  • Silent Spring (1962) by Rachel Carson - An influential piece of environmental literature that led to environmental safeguards and bans on harmful chemicals like DDT.
  • The Tragedy of the Commons (1968) by Garrett Hardin - Published in response to accelerating natural degradation.
  • National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) (1969) - A US law requiring analysis of federal government actions on the environment, leading to the creation of Environmental Impact Statements.
  • Montreal Protocol (1987) - Global agreement inspired by the discovery of the ozone hole, banning the use of chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs).
  • Paris Agreement (2016) - Successor to the Kyoto Protocol, setting goals to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and limit the Earth's temperature rise.
