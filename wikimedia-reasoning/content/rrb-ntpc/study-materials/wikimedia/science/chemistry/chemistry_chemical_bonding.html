Chemical Bond

Chemical bonds are associations of atoms or ions forming molecules, crystals, and other structures. They arise from electrostatic forces between oppositely charged particles or electron sharing. Chemical bonds are categorized into strong (primary) bonds like covalent, ionic, and metallic, and weak (secondary) bonds such as dipole–dipole interactions, London dispersion forces, and hydrogen bonding.

Strong Chemical Bonds

Ionic Bond

Definition: Electrostatic interaction between atoms with a large electronegativity difference, involving the transfer of electrons.

Characteristics:

  • Electron is transferred, not shared.
  • One atom gains electron(s) (anion), becoming negatively charged.
  • One atom loses electron(s) (cation), becoming positively charged.
  • Bond results from electrostatic attraction between positive and negative ions.
  • Electronegativity difference > 1.7 (guideline).
  • Often non-directional in space, due to equal attraction to surrounding ions.
  • Strong bonds, resulting in high melting points.
  • Brittle, as forces are short-range and do not easily bridge cracks.
  • Typically forms ionic crystals where no ion is specifically paired to another.

Examples:

  • Sodium chloride (table salt, NaCl)
  • Sodium cyanide (NaCN, ionic between Na+ and CN-)

Covalent Bond

Definition: Sharing of valence electrons between two or more atoms.

Characteristics:

  • One or more electron pairs are shared between atomic nuclei.
  • Energy is released upon bond formation (due to reduction in kinetic energy as electrons are more spatially distributed).
  • Exist between specific, identifiable atoms.
  • Have a direction in space.
  • Form molecules, which can be bound by weaker intermolecular forces.
  • Can form strong and tough structures if chains/networks are formed (e.g., polymers, diamond).

Types of Covalent Bonds:

Non-polar Covalent Bond:

  • Electrons shared equally or almost equally.
  • Electronegativity difference: 0 to 0.3
  • Often immiscible in water/polar solvents, more soluble in non-polar solvents.
  • Example: Methane (CH4)

Polar Covalent Bond:

  • Electrons shared unequally, creating a charge imbalance.
  • Electronegativity difference: 0.3 to 1.7
  • Exhibits significant ionic character, leads to dipole–dipole interactions.

Single Bond: Sharing of one pair of electrons (two electrons). Example: H–H in hydrogen molecule (H2)

Double Bond: Sharing of two pairs of electrons (four electrons), consisting of one sigma bond and one pi bond.

Triple Bond: Sharing of three pairs of electrons (six electrons), consisting of one sigma bond and two pi bonds. Example: N≡N in nitrogen molecule

Quadruple and Higher Bonds: Very rare, observed between certain transition metal atoms.

Coordinate Covalent Bond: A covalent bond where both shared bonding electrons come from only one of the atoms involved. Example: Adduct of ammonia and boron trifluoride (F3B←NH3)

Metallic Bonding

Definition: Bonding where electrons are delocalized over a lattice of atoms, forming a 'sea of electrons'.

Characteristics:

  • Each metal atom donates one or more electrons to a collective 'sea'.
  • Electrons are free to be associated with many atoms at once (wave nature).
  • Metal atoms become positively charged, attracted to the delocalized electron sea.
  • Considered an extreme example of electron delocalization in covalent bonds.
  • Often very strong bonds.
  • Collective nature allows metal crystals to deform easily (malleability, ductility).
  • Delocalized electron cloud enables good electrical and thermal conductivity.
  • Causes characteristic shiny luster (reflects most frequencies of white light).

Weak Chemical Bonds

Weak bonds include:

  • Dipole–dipole interactions
  • London dispersion force
  • Hydrogen bonding

Typical Bond Properties

The properties and types of bonds are fundamentally explained by quantum theory, with simplified models like the octet rule and VSEPR theory, and more sophisticated theories including valence bond theory and molecular orbital theory. The type of strong bond is largely determined by the electronegativity difference between constituent elements.
