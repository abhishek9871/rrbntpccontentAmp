Periodic Table

The periodic table is an ordered arrangement of chemical elements into rows (periods) and columns (groups), widely used in chemistry, physics, and other sciences. It depicts the periodic law.

Basic Properties

  • Elements Known: 118
  • Naturally occurring elements: 94
  • Primordial elements: 83
  • Elements from decay chains: 11
  • Synthesized elements: 24

Structure and Organization

Basic Arrangement

Elements arranged in order of increasing atomic number (Z). Rows are called periods, and columns are called groups. Four blocks (s, p, d, f) correspond to the subshells being filled by the outermost electrons.

Periods (Rows)

Horizontal rows indicating the principal quantum number (n) of the outermost electron shell being filled.

  • Period 1: 2 elements (1s orbital)
  • Period 2: 8 elements (2s, 2p orbitals)
  • Period 3: 8 elements (3s, 3p orbitals)
  • Period 4: 18 elements (4s, 3d, 4p orbitals)
  • Period 5: 18 elements (5s, 4d, 5p orbitals)
  • Period 6: 32 elements (6s, 4f, 5d, 6p orbitals)
  • Period 7: 32 elements (7s, 5f, 6d, 7p orbitals)

Groups (Columns)

Elements with similar outer electron configurations and chemical characteristics. IUPAC numbering: 1 to 18 (left to right).

Blocks

  • s-block: Groups 1, 2, and Helium (filling s-orbitals)
  • p-block: Groups 13-18 (filling p-orbitals)
  • d-block: Groups 3-12 (filling d-orbitals), also called transition elements
  • f-block: Lanthanides and Actinides (filling f-orbitals)

The Periodic Law

States that when elements are arranged in order of their atomic numbers, an approximate recurrence of their properties is evident.

Historical Development

  • 1869: Dmitri Mendeleev formulated first periodic table based on atomic mass, predicted properties of missing elements
  • 1913: Antonius van den Broek proposed nuclear charge (atomic number) determines element placement
  • 1913: Henry Moseley confirmed atomic number placement using X-ray spectroscopy

Periodic Trends

Atomic Radius

Across period: Generally decreases left to right (increasing nuclear charge, same shell)
Down group: Generally increases top to bottom (outermost electrons in higher shells)

Ionization Energy

Definition: Energy required to remove an electron from an atom.

Trends:

  • Increases left to right across periods
  • Increases bottom to top down groups
  • Minimum for first element of each period (H, alkali metals)
  • Maximum for noble gases

Electron Affinity

Definition: Energy released when adding an electron to an atom.

Trends:

  • Tends to increase left to right across periods
  • Tends to increase bottom to top down groups
  • Noble gases have no electron affinity (full shell)

Electronegativity

Definition: Tendency of an atom to attract a shared electron pair in a covalent bond.

Trends:

  • Rises left to right across periods
  • Falls top to bottom down groups
  • Most electropositive: Alkali and alkaline earth metals
  • Most electronegative: Fluorine (4.0 on Pauling scale)
  • Least electronegative: Caesium (0.79 on Pauling scale)

Metallicity

Metals: Shiny, dense, high melting/boiling points, malleable, ductile, good conductors

Nonmetals: Dull-looking, brittle (when solid), poor electrical conductors

Semiconductors: Elements with small band gaps (Si, Ge, Te, black P, grey Se)

Oxidation States

Main group elements: Valences relate directly to group number

d-block elements: Common oxidation states typically +2 or higher

f-block elements: Lanthanides and late actinides generally show stable +3
